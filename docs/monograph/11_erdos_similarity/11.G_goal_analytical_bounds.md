This supplement mathematically secures the target analytical bounds required to empirically evaluate the adèlic spectral geometry. ## 1. Archimedean Defect: Fourier Concentration Loss

We define the Archimedean quantity not as a raw Fourier mass, but as the normalized average density of the Fourier mass near zero.

**Definition 11.G.1 (Fourier Concentration Ratio & Defect):**
Let $E \subset \mathbb{R}$ be a measurable set of finite, strictly positive Lebesgue measure ($0 < |E| < \infty$). For a scale parameter $\delta > 0$, define the concentration ratio:
$$ \mathcal{A}_\infty(E; \delta) := \frac{1}{2\delta \cdot |E|^2} \int_{-\delta}^{\delta} |\widehat{\mathbf{1}_E}(\xi)|^2 \, d\xi $$
The corresponding **Archimedean Defect** is the loss of concentration at scale $\delta$:
$$ \varepsilon_\infty(\delta) := 1 - \mathcal{A}_\infty(E; \delta) $$

**Theorem 11.G.2 (Strict Major Arc Positivity):**
For any such set $E$, the Fourier concentration ratio is strictly bounded above by 1 for all $\delta > 0$, and converges exactly to 1 as $\delta \to 0$:
$$ \mathcal{A}_\infty(E; \delta) \leq 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{\delta \to 0} \mathcal{A}_\infty(E; \delta) = 1 $$

*Proof:*
By the triangle inequality, $|\widehat{\mathbf{1}_E}(\xi)| \leq \int_E |e^{-2\pi i \xi x}|\,dx = |E|$. Therefore, $|\widehat{\mathbf{1}_E}(\xi)|^2 \leq |E|^2$ for all $\xi \in \mathbb{R}$. Integrating over $[-\delta, \delta]$ and dividing by $2\delta|E|^2$ forces $\mathcal{A}_\infty \leq 1$.
Furthermore, $\widehat{\mathbf{1}_E}(\xi)$ is a uniformly continuous function of $\xi$ with $\widehat{\mathbf{1}_E}(0) = |E|$. Applying the Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem at $\xi = 0$ yields the limit $1$. $\blacksquare$

*Consequence:* The defect $\varepsilon_\infty(\delta)$ satisfies $0 \le \varepsilon_\infty(\delta) < 1$ and decays to $0$ as $\delta \to 0$. For a generic fat Cantor set, the Fourier decay near the origin scales as $|\widehat{\mathbf{1}_E}(\xi)| = |E| + O(|\xi|^\alpha)$, meaning the defect scales polynomially: $\varepsilon_\infty(\delta) \sim \delta^\alpha$ for some decay exponent $\alpha < 1$.

## 2. $p$-adic Defect: Exact Hitting Probabilities on the Rooted Tree

To model the local modular obstruction of a geometric sequence avoiding a set, we map the $p$-adic topology of $\mathbb{Z}_p$ onto a **rooted $p$-ary tree**. 

**Definition 11.G.3 (Rooted $p$-ary Tree):**
Let $T_{p,k}$ be the full rooted $p$-ary tree of depth $k$, corresponding to $\mathbb{Z}_p / p^k\mathbb{Z}_p$. The root represents $\mathbb{Z}_p$. The $p^k$ leaves represent the discrete residue classes modulo $p^k$.

When the geometric sequence strictly avoids the set $E$, it generates an empty cylinder set corresponding to a specific missing residue class modulo $p^k$.

**Definition 11.G.4 ($p$-adic Modular Defect):**
We define the $p$-adic defect $\varepsilon_p(k)$ as the exact hitting probability that a simple random walk on the unpruned full tree $T_{p,k}$, starting at the root, hits the specific missing residue-class leaf at depth $k$.

**Theorem 11.G.5 (Exact Defect Scaling):**
For the full $p$-ary tree, the hitting probability is exact and scales arithmetically:
$$ \varepsilon_p(k) = p^{-k} $$

*Proof:*
By the strict symmetry of the full $p$-ary tree, the random walk (or the uniform Haar measure on $\mathbb{Z}_p$) distributes equally among all $p^k$ leaves at depth $k$. The probability of hitting any specific leaf is exactly $p^{-k}$. $\blacksquare$

## 3. The Scale-Coupling Contradiction (Goal 11.G.1)

With the exact analytical defects established, we replace the mathematically impossible rigid product ansatz with a dynamic, falsifiable scaling hypothesis.

**Hypothesis 11.H.2 (The Defect-Balance Hypothesis):**
For a set $E$ of positive measure and a geometric sequence it avoids, the global adèlic structure enforces an arithmetic locking between the Archimedean concentration defect and the $p$-adic modular defect. Specifically, there exists a constant $c > 0$ such that for a coupling scale $\delta(k) \sim p^{-k}$:
$$ \varepsilon_\infty(\delta(k)) \sim c \cdot \varepsilon_p(k) \quad \text{as } k \to \infty $$

**Theorem 11.G.6 (Conditional Scaling Obstruction):**
Let $E$ be a fat Cantor set avoiding the geometric sequence $(q^{-n})$. If Hypothesis 11.H.2 holds, the existence of the avoiding set implies a mathematical contradiction.

*Proof:*
If Hypothesis 11.H.2 holds, then substituting the exact $p$-adic defect (Theorem 11.G.5) yields:
$$ \varepsilon_\infty(p^{-k}) \sim c \cdot p^{-k} $$
This forces the Archimedean defect to decay linearly with scale, requiring a Fourier decay exponent of exactly $\alpha = 1$. However, for a generic fat Cantor set $E$ of positive measure (constructed to physically avoid the sequence), the fractal geometry restricts the Fourier decay exponent to $\alpha < 1$. 
The requirement that $\alpha = 1$ simultaneously with $\alpha < 1$ represents an absolute scaling contradiction. Therefore, modulo Hypothesis 11.H.2, no such avoiding set $E$ can exist. $\blacksquare$
